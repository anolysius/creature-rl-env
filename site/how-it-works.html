How the exam works

The mechanics behind the leaderboard — the win condition, the hidden type chart, and why there is no grinding. Engine facts only; measured results (and their caveats) live in the repository.

What does it take to beat a gym?

Step onto a gym tile and a boss battle starts (your party enters fully healed). The win condition is simple: reduce the boss's HP to 0 before your whole party faints (a turn cap ends stalemates as a draw). Both sides act every turn, the faster side hits first, and there are no dice — the same choices always produce the same battle.

Per-turn damage is roughly move power × (attack ÷ defense) × matchup, and the matchup multiplier is ×2 / ×1 / ×0.5 (super-effective / neutral / resisted) — a 4× swing between the right and wrong pick. Bosses are tuned tanky, so that multiplier effectively decides the damage race: the right creature wins it, the wrong one loses it.

The type chart is hidden — and re-rolled every world

You can see the boss's type id, but what beats it is re-rolled per world seed and never shown. Memorizing a fixed FIRE>GRASS>WATER meta is useless here — the only route is to attack, watch how much HP actually drops, and infer the matchup from the observation. World generation guarantees every boss has at least one counter in your starter party — in the default exam; the hidden-secondary knob can weaken this — so the exam stays solvable in principle.

How hard is finding the counter?

The raw search is deliberately small: three starters, one move each, and a single hit reveals one cell of the answer (the damage number tells you super / neutral / resisted). Worst case, two or three probes solve one boss type. The difficulty is engineered into the cost structure of those experiments instead:

Difficulty axisWhat it does
Probing is not freeWhile you test a wrong creature, the boss is hitting back — and every step of the episode budget you spend probing is a step not spent exploring.
The ledger growsWhat you must remember scales with the number of distinct boss types in the world × your party — from trivial (one recurring type) to real bookkeeping (many).
Commit mode (opt-in knob)No switching once the fight starts — you must pick your champion before the battle, from memory alone. In-battle probing is gone.
Hidden secondary types (opt-in knob)A boss may carry a hidden second type; multipliers multiply, so one observation no longer cleanly identifies the matchup.
Strict economies (opt-in knobs)Variants zero out resisted (or even neutral) damage — the wrong answer stops chipping and the right pick becomes the only path to a win.

Why you can't grind your way to a win

Several rules look odd if you expect a creature-collection RPG. They are not arbitrary — each one closes a specific “win without understanding” shortcut, the way a math exam bans calculators and open books. One principle: the only reliable way to win is to infer the hidden chart.

RuleShortcut it closes
No wild battles / no XP farmingOut-leveling the problem instead of out-thinking it.
Catching never adds battle strengthCollect-to-win — patience substituting for inference.
Levels come only from winningPre-grinding before a challenge; strength is a consequence of solving, never a substitute.
Rematches are free but deterministicRetry-until-lucky — a rematch only helps if you change your strategy.

What this exam measures — and deliberately does NOT

Measures: long-horizon planning under a small egocentric view, inferring a hidden, per-world type chart from observed damage (experiment → observe → remember → reuse), and generalization to unseen worlds.

Deliberately does not measure: RPG-style resource and progression management. There is no wild grinding, catching never adds battle strength, and levels only come from winning — every “win without understanding” shortcut is closed on purpose, like banning calculators in a math exam.

This scope is a choice, not an accident: adding an RPG economy (resources, preparation, grinding decisions) would reopen the very attrition shortcuts the rules above close. Harder variants exist as opt-in knobs instead, so the default exam stays a clean measure of inference.
